## Executive summary – SECURE financing model

### Purpose

**SECURE** is a GARDP-led initiative designed to **ensure sustainable access to essential antibiotics in LMICs while enforcing responsible use** (stewardship). The report explores **how to finance SECURE at scale**, blending public, country, and private capital.

---

## Problem statement

- **Antimicrobial resistance (AMR)** is rising rapidly, causing ~700,000 deaths annually (projected up to 10m/year by 2050).
- The **antibiotics business model is broken**:
    - R&D is underfunded.
    - Commercialization is unsustainable due to stewardship-driven low volumes.
- Existing access programs are **donor-dependent**, which is increasingly untenable.

---

## What SECURE does

SECURE acts as an **access enabler** across the full value chain:

1. Regulatory support and faster registration
2. Evidence generation and guideline development
3. Demand forecasting and pooled procurement
4. Country capacity building (stewardship, systems)
5. Provision of quality, affordable antibiotics (new + established)

Operationally, SECURE is built around **pooled procurement and coordinated supply-chain integration**, similar to Gavi / Global Fund–style models.

---

## Scale and cost

**Indicative annual costs** (country size ~60m people):

### Pilot phase (5 countries)

- Programme & operations: **~$30–40m/year**
- Antibiotic provision: **~$7m/country/year**
- Total: **~$69m/year**

### Full implementation (20 countries)

- Programme & operations: **~$36m/year**
- Antibiotic provision: **~$10m/country/year**
- Total: **~$238m/year**

---

## Core financing insight

**Countries ultimately pay for antibiotics**, but:

- **Upfront costs and risks block adoption**, especially in pilots.
- **Public/philanthropic funding must be catalytic**, not permanent.

Hence the need for **results-based pre-financing**.

---

## Proposed financing model (core innovation)

### 1. Roles by funder type

- **Development funders (G7, multilaterals, philanthropies)**
    - Fund “global public goods” (secretariat, evidence, registration)
    - Provide **first-loss guarantees** and **subsidies for investor returns**
- **Investors (DFIs, MDBs, impact investors)**
    - Provide **results-based pre-financing** for antibiotic provision
    - Paid back by countries _if and when results are achieved_
- **Beneficiary countries**
    - Pay for antibiotics over time
    - Gradually co-finance programme components
- **Antibiotic manufacturers**
    - Supply at preferential prices
    - Benefit from predictable demand and regulatory support

---

### 2. Pilot phase financing

- Countries receive antibiotics **at no upfront cost**
- Antibiotic provision pre-financed via **concessional loans / repayable grants**
- Repayment triggered by **process indicators** (e.g. delivery timeliness, registration, quality)
- Donors absorb early risk

> Objective: **prove the model, reduce country risk, build confidence**

---

### 3. Implementation phase financing

- Countries transition to:
    - **Direct payment or subscription-style models** for antibiotics
    - **Tiered contributions** to forecasting, procurement, and capacity building
- Investors continue to support _new_ countries joining
- Donor role shrinks to guarantees, co-payments for LICs, and public goods

> Result: **declining donor share, rising country ownership**

---

## Key financial instruments

- Results-based concessional loans
- First-loss guarantees (≈50% of principal)
- Grants to pay investor premiums
- Possible **subscription / partially de-linked models** for antibiotics

---

## Value proposition

### For countries

- Faster, reliable access to quality antibiotics
- Lower prices via pooled procurement
- Regulatory and stewardship support
- Ability to “try before paying” during early years

### For development funders

- Large-scale AMR impact with **limited catalytic funding**
- Strong leverage of private capital
- Improved sustainability vs grant-heavy models

### For investors

- Impact-aligned returns (≈5% indicative)
- Risk mitigation via guarantees and pooled country exposure

---

## Evidence on value for money

A high-level value assessment suggests:

- **Social and economic benefits exceed costs**, particularly in MICs
- Significant gains through:
    - Avoided mortality
    - Saved productivity (GDP)
    - Reduced hospitalization costs

---

## Beyond SECURE: stewardship enablers

- Diagnostics, surveillance tools, and prescribing decision support are **critical but underfunded**
- SECURE scale could catalyze demand
- Report recommends **blended-finance vehicles** (existing or new funds) to support these tools

---

## Key conclusions

- SECURE is **financially feasible** if designed as a **blended, results-based platform**
- Donor funding should be **temporary and catalytic**
- Country payment + investor pre-financing is essential for sustainability
- The model is **analogous to APAs / AMCs**, adapted for antibiotics and stewardship

- **older essential antibiotics** that are in shortage/not widely available, and

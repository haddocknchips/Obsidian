### Notes form M365CP

- **Innovative Financial Mechanisms for Product Access:** Yann Ferrisse and Paolo discussed the exploration and implementation of innovative financial mechanisms, such as volume guarantees, concessional financing, risk sharing, and pooled procurement, to address bottlenecks in sustainable supply, affordability, and scale-up for products like Zolifidacin and Cephid Oracle, with reference to ongoing collaborations with Med Access, Global Fund, and consulting teams.
    - **Volume Guarantee Approaches:** Yann Ferrisse explained the need for volume guarantee or market guarantee mechanisms to support commercial partners like Dr. Reddy in launching new antibiotics, particularly Zolifidacin, due to high transfer prices and low initial volumes, referencing previous successes in HIV product launches with Global Fund support.
    - **Financial Modelling Constraints:** Yann Ferrisse highlighted two main constraints in financial modelling: uncertainty around target transfer prices and the limited willingness of organisations like Med Access and Global Fund to engage in volume guarantees for products addressing gonorrhea, which are less politically attractive compared to HIV.
    - **Pooled Procurement and Market Shaping:** Both participants discussed the role of pooled procurement as a market-shaping mechanism, noting ongoing efforts with African CDC and Indian partners, but also expressing uncertainty about its effectiveness as a financial mechanism.
    - **Advance Purchase Options:** Yann Ferrisse described the consideration of advance purchase agreements for validation vials or commercial batches as a potential mechanism to support manufacturing and clinical activities, though internal consensus on this approach is not yet established.
    - **Collaboration with Med Access:** Yann Ferrisse confirmed ongoing discussions with Med Access, mentioning contacts like Max Sullivan, to explore financial risk-sharing and backing for market guarantees, though engagement remains challenging due to product focus and organisational priorities.
- **GARDP Portfolio and Operating Models:** Yann Ferrisse provided Paolo with a comprehensive overview of GARDP's portfolio, including product archetypes, commercial operating models, and the strategic approach to research, development, and access, highlighting the complexities of managing intellectual property, manufacturing rights, and global access across diverse geographies.
    - **Product Archetypes:** Yann Ferrisse described several portfolio archetypes: products fully managed by GARDP from clinical development to manufacturing, co-development projects with partners, enabling and implementing projects with indirect influence, and neonatal sepsis programmes focused on repurposing existing products.
    - **Commercial Operating Models:** The discussion covered models where GARDP holds patents or licence rights, partners with commercial entities, and facilitates access ecosystems, noting varying levels of leverage and responsibility depending on the product and agreement structure.
    - **Global Access and Licensing:** Yann Ferrisse explained the broad scope of GARDP's licensing, covering low-, middle-, and some high-income countries, with case-by-case variations depending on product and partner interests, and ongoing efforts to renegotiate access in challenging geographies like China and Latin America.
    - **Tiered Pricing and Royalties:** GARDP employs tiered pricing strategies by country and sector, negotiating with commercial partners to ensure business viability while maintaining affordability and requesting royalties, with mechanisms to avoid negative impacts on early access.
- **Challenges in Commercialisation and Market Entry:** Yann Ferrisse detailed the commercialisation challenges faced with products like Zolifidacin and Cephid Oracle, including unattractive business cases for partners, high cost of goods, limited market data, and manufacturing constraints, and discussed strategies to overcome these barriers through regulatory milestones, managed access programmes, and partnerships.
    - **Business Model Limitations:** Yann Ferrisse described how Dr. Reddy, despite supporting regulatory approval for Zolifidacin in Thailand, is unwilling to launch the product due to insufficient revenue visibility, high transfer prices, and low patient volumes, resulting in a lack of commercial batches.
    - **Cost of Goods and Price Sensitivity:** The team conducted health economic assessments and price sensitivity analyses, revealing a significant gap between the cost of goods (around $40) and the market's willingness to pay ($10-20), with existing generic products priced at $1, highlighting the challenge of introducing new oral antibiotics.
    - **Regulatory and Manufacturing Constraints:** For Cephid Oracle, Yann Ferrisse explained the reliance on tech transfer managed by Chai, the difficulty in financing CapEx due to tax implications, and manufacturing limitations at Shinogi's Japanese facility, affecting supply and distribution plans.
    - **Managed Access and Introduction Studies:** GARDP is developing managed access programmes and introduction studies in India, South Africa, Brazil, and Kenya, using validation batches or products from Shinogi or Orchid, with ongoing negotiations regarding donations or cost-based purchases.
- **Internal Coordination and Strategic Alignment:** Yann Ferrisse and Paolo discussed the importance of aligning innovative finance initiatives with existing GARDP projects and teams, clarifying roles, avoiding duplication, and ensuring that new ideas supplement rather than replace ongoing work, with plans to share documents and reports for Paolo's onboarding.
    - **Role Clarification:** Yann Ferrisse questioned the internal organisation regarding Jeff's involvement in product-related financial mechanisms, emphasising the need for clear coordination between teams working on sustainability, access, and affordability.
    - **Onboarding and Knowledge Sharing:** Yann Ferrisse agreed to share presentations, reports, and contract examples with Paolo, noting that the Delhi-based team is more operational, and suggesting Paolo use SharePoint for access to relevant documents.
- **Strategic Considerations for Manufacturing Investment:** Yann Ferrisse and Paolo explored the strategic implications of GARDP investing in manufacturing capacity, including governance, financing, and operational challenges, referencing board reluctance and the need for careful validation before committing to such a shift.
    - **Board Reluctance and Governance:** Yann Ferrisse recounted board discussions, particularly with German funding representatives, expressing reluctance to invest in manufacturing upgrades due to the significant commitment and potential shift in GARDP's organisational responsibilities.
    - **Operational Capacity:** The conversation highlighted that GARDP's CMC team is focused on research and development, not commercial manufacturing, and that expanding into manufacturing would require substantial increases in capacity and expertise.
- **SECURE Project and Policy Tool Limitations:** Yann Ferrisse described the SECURE project, developed with WHO and other partners, as a policy-maker tool facing implementation challenges, including difficulties in country selection and stakeholder engagement, contrasting it with more actionable product-focused projects.
    - **Implementation Challenges:** Yann Ferrisse explained that SECURE, despite having a business plan and pilot proposal, struggles with WHO's consultation requirements and lack of decisive country selection, leading to project stagnation.
    - **Policy Versus Actionable Projects:** The distinction was made between SECURE as a policy-shaping tool and projects like Safie and Zuli, which are outcome-oriented and actionable, helping Paolo understand the different roles and expectations for each initiative.

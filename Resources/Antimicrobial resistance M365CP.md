when humans and animals excrete antibiotics after taking or ingesting them, how altered are the drugs? do they pose any risk of contributing to antimicrobial resistance?

Response  

Great question—this sits at the intersection of pharmacology, environmental science, and antimicrobial resistance (AMR).

Short answer: **many antibiotics are excreted only partially altered, and yes, their excretion _does_ contribute to antimicrobial resistance**, especially once they enter wastewater, soil, and aquatic ecosystems. Below is a clear breakdown of _how altered the drugs are_ and _why they matter for resistance_.

---

## 1. How altered are antibiotics when excreted?

### a. A large fraction are excreted **unchanged or still biologically active**

Many commonly used antibiotics are **poorly metabolized by humans and animals**. Depending on the drug class, **~30–90% of the administered dose may be excreted as the parent compound** (unchanged) in urine or feces, or as metabolites that retain antimicrobial activity. [[medxdrg.com]](https://medxdrg.com/what-is-the-fate-of-antibiotics-understanding-the-journey-from-prescription-to-pollution), [[medxdrg.com]](https://medxdrg.com/what-is-the-excretion-rate-of-antibiotics-understanding-how-the-body-clears-medications)

**Examples:**

- **β-lactams** (e.g., amoxicillin): large fractions excreted unchanged via kidneys
- **Fluoroquinolones** (e.g., ciprofloxacin): partially metabolized, but active parent drug persists
- **Tetracyclines & sulfonamides**: limited metabolism; substantial active residues
- **Macrolides** (e.g., azithromycin): excreted slowly; metabolites may remain active

Pharmacokinetic studies show that for many antibiotics, renal clearance dominates over metabolic breakdown, especially for hydrophilic drugs. [[pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6625637/), [[scholarwor...lis.iu.edu]](https://scholarworks.indianapolis.iu.edu/server/api/core/bitstreams/9c0c9a57-5736-4567-a551-6eec7f99d7ef/content)

---

### b. Metabolites are not necessarily “inactive”

Even when antibiotics are metabolized, the resulting compounds are **not always harmless**. Some metabolites:

- Retain partial antibacterial activity
- Can revert to the active parent compound in the environment
- Still exert selective pressure on bacteria

Environmental chemistry reviews confirm that **both original antibiotics and metabolites persist in water and soil** and must be considered together when assessing risk. [[mdpi.com]](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/9/5182), [[link.springer.com]](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-04598-0_3)

---

## 2. What happens after excretion?

### a. Wastewater treatment does **not** reliably remove antibiotics

Human antibiotics are mostly routed through wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs), while animal antibiotics enter the environment via **manure, lagoons, or agricultural runoff**.

However:

- Conventional WWTPs are **not designed to eliminate pharmaceuticals**
- Removal efficiencies vary widely by drug
- Some treatment processes may even **enrich antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs)** rather than remove them

As a result, antibiotics and resistant bacteria are routinely detected in:

- Effluent water
- River sediments
- Soil irrigated with reclaimed water. [[link.springer.com]](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-04598-0_3), [[nature.com]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-35758-1)

---

### b. Livestock manure is a major pathway

In animals, antibiotics are excreted at similar or higher rates than in humans. When manure is applied to fields:

- Antibiotic residues persist in soil
- Resistance genes accumulate
- Resistant bacteria can transfer genes to native microbes

Manure-amended soils consistently show higher abundances of **clinically relevant resistance genes**, including those for tetracyclines, sulfonamides, and fluoroquinolones. [[pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9522911/), [[mdpi.com]](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4441/17/20/2960)

---

## 3. Do excreted antibiotics contribute to antimicrobial resistance?

### Yes—through several well-established mechanisms.

#### a. **Sub-inhibitory selection pressure**

Environmental antibiotic concentrations are often too low to kill bacteria outright, but **high enough to select for resistance**. These “sub-MIC” levels:

- Favor survival of resistant strains
- Promote mutations
- Increase stress responses that accelerate evolution

This selective pressure is now widely recognized as a **key driver of environmental AMR**. [[link.springer.com]](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-04598-0_3), [[academic.oup.com]](https://academic.oup.com/jac/article/52/1/5/929958)

---

#### b. **Enhanced horizontal gene transfer**

Low antibiotic concentrations increase:

- Plasmid transfer
- Integron activity
- Mobilization of resistance genes between species

Studies show that antibiotic residues, even at trace levels, stimulate **horizontal gene transfer**, accelerating the spread of AMR across microbial communities. [[link.springer.com]](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-04598-0_3), [[nature.com]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-35758-1)

---

#### c. **Creation of environmental resistance reservoirs**

Once resistance genes establish themselves in soil, sediments, and water:

- They persist long-term
- They can re-enter human and animal populations via food, water, or contact
- They act as a “background reservoir” that undermines clinical antibiotic effectiveness

This environmental dimension is now central to the **One Health** framework for AMR. [[link.springer.com]](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-04598-0_3), [[nature.com]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01155-0)

---

## 4. Are all antibiotics equally risky?

No. Risk depends on:

- Fraction excreted unchanged
- Environmental persistence
- Spectrum of activity
- Frequency of use

Antibiotics most strongly associated with environmental AMR include:

- Tetracyclines
- Sulfonamides
- Fluoroquinolones
- Macrolides

These are widely used, poorly degraded, and commonly detected outside the body. [[pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9522911/), [[medxdrg.com]](https://medxdrg.com/what-is-the-fate-of-antibiotics-understanding-the-journey-from-prescription-to-pollution)

---

## 5. Bottom line

**How altered are excreted antibiotics?**

- Often **not very**—many retain antimicrobial activity
- Even metabolites can remain biologically relevant

**Do they contribute to antimicrobial resistance?**

- **Yes, clearly and measurably**
- By selecting for resistant bacteria
- By promoting gene transfer
- By creating long-lived environmental reservoirs of resistance

This is why antimicrobial stewardship, improved waste treatment, and reduced unnecessary antibiotic use in both medicine and agriculture are all critical for slowing AMR.

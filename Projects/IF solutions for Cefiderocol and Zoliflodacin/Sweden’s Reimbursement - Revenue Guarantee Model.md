### What it is

Sweden operates a **government‑backed revenue guarantee model** to ensure availability of antibiotics with **high medical value but low commercial demand**. Piloted from 2018 onwards. [[ec.europa.eu]](https://ec.europa.eu/assets/sante/health/amr/docs/amr_20240301_co09_en.pdf), [[folkhalsom...igheten.se]](https://www.folkhalsomyndigheten.se/the-public-health-agency-of-sweden/communicable-disease-control/antibiotics-and-antimicrobial-resistance/overview-of-swedens-one-health-response-to-antibiotic-resistance/exploring-swedens-approach-to-antibiotic-resistance-in-the-human-health-sector/availability-of-antibiotics/)

### Core features

- Government guarantees a **minimum annual revenue**
- Includes:
    - Stocking obligations
    - Buffer inventory requirements
- Hospitals still pay per use, but:
    - State tops up revenue shortfalls

### Pull‑mechanism characteristics

- **Partial delinkage**
- Best described as an **availability pull**, not an R&D incentive
- Highly compatible with stewardship

### Explicit limitation (by design)

- **Not intended to incentivise early‑stage R&D**
- Other pull and push mechanisms are required in parallel [[ec.europa.eu]](https://ec.europa.eu/assets/sante/health/amr/docs/amr_20240301_co09_en.pdf)

**Bottom line:** Sweden solves the**“keep the drug on the market” problem**, not the global innovation problem.
